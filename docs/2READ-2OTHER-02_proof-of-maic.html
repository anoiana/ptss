Let \(\boldsymbol{p} = {(p_1,p_2,\dots,p_n)}^{\top}\) be propensity score and \(\boldsymbol{X} = {(\boldsymbol{x}_1,\boldsymbol{x}_2, \dots, \boldsymbol{x}_n)}^{\top}\) be baseline characteristics of subjects in IPD. Also, \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) denotes average vector of baseline characteristics found in SLD. We aim to estimate \(\boldsymbol{p}\) such that \({\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X} = {\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{\top}\), this is, we want to minimize Kullback Leibler divergence of \(\boldsymbol{p}\) and empirical function with some constraints. Specifically, we want to minimize \[ -{\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\ln(\frac{1}{n\boldsymbol{p}}), \] with two constraints: \[ {\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{1} = 1, \quad and \quad {\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X} = {\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{\top} \]

We shall use Lagrange Multiplier to solve such an optimization problem as follows.

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{L} &= -{\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\ln(\frac{1}{n\boldsymbol{p}})\boldsymbol{1} - \lambda{({\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X} - {\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{\top})}^{\top} \\ &= {\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\ln(n\boldsymbol{p})\boldsymbol{1} - \lambda[( {\boldsymbol{X}}^{\top} - \boldsymbol{\mu}{\boldsymbol{1}}^{\top} )\boldsymbol{p}]. \end{aligned} \tag{1} \]

The solution is to take derivative w.r.t \(\boldsymbol{p}\) and \(\lambda\), equate both derivations to zero and solve both equations. First, Taking derivative \(\mathcal{L}\) w.r.t \(\lambda\) we obtain \[ {\boldsymbol{p}}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X} = {\boldsymbol{\mu}}^{\top}. \tag{2} \] Next, taking derivative w.r.t \(p_i\): \[ \begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial p_i} = 0 \\ &\Rightarrow \ln(np_i) +n -\lambda(x_i-\mu) = 0 \\ &\Rightarrow \ln(np_i) = \lambda(x_i-\mu)-n \\ &\Rightarrow p_i = \frac{\exp[\lambda(x_i -\mu)-n]}{n} \\ &\Rightarrow p_i = \frac{e^{-n}}{n}\exp[\lambda(x_i-\mu)] \end{aligned} \tag{3} \] thus, \[ \begin{aligned} &\quad 1 = \sum_{i=1}^np_i = \frac{e^{-n}}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\exp[\lambda(x_i-\mu)] \\ & \Rightarrow \frac{n}{e^{-n}} = \sum_{i=1}^n\exp[\lambda(x_i-\mu)] \end{aligned} \tag{4} \] substitute Eq. (4) to Eq. (3), we obtain

\[ p_i = \frac{\exp[\lambda(x_i - \mu)]}{\sum_{i=1}^n\exp[\lambda(x_i-\mu)]} \tag{5} \]

Eq. (5) indicates that \(p_i\) can be estimated by fitting logistic model in which \(z_i= x_i-\mu\) is one covariate, and \(z_i\) must be statisfying Eq. (2).
